Plan-Do-Check-Act

The Deming Cycle: improvement through experimentation, observation, learning, and adjustment.

Plan
Define a change or experiment. Identify what to improve and how to measure success.
Do
Execute the change. Implement on a small scale to test the hypothesis.
Check
Examine outcomes and learn. Did the change produce expected results?
Act
Standardize improvements or adjust direction. Iterate and begin again.
The universal feedback loop

Systems thinking, statistical quality control, and continuous improvement through experimentation.

The Problem
05 / 20

Experimentation Was Expensive

Every step in the improvement loop required human effort, coordination, and time.

Execute tests manually
Engineers wrote, ran, and monitored every experiment by hand.
Analyze data manually
Results required human interpretation, often days or weeks later.
Coordinate communication
Sharing findings required meetings, documents, and alignment.
Apply changes manually
Every improvement had to be implemented step by step.
The feedback loop was measured in weeks, not seconds

Plan: Human Intent

Planning remains fundamentally human because it requires capabilities AI cannot replicate.

Contextual understanding
Grasping the full situation and its nuances
Ethical judgment
Deciding what should vs. shouldn't be done
Strategic prioritization
Choosing what matters most given constraints
Long-term thinking
Considering consequences beyond the immediate
Humans determine: What matters. What success means. Which risks are acceptable.
AI can assist, but humans define purpose

AI systems can execute changes at machine speed, running thousands of experiments in parallel.

Speed
Execute thousands of variations while humans complete one. Parallel experimentation at scale.
Consistency
No fatigue, no variation, no human error. Every execution follows protocol precisely.
Scale
Handle complexity that would overwhelm human teams. Coordinate across systems seamlessly.

The Over-Automation Trap

If AI handles all four stages, the cycle becomes a black box. The organization loses understanding.

Without human involvement in planning and interpretation, organizations optimize for metrics without understanding context. They become faster at doing the wrong things.

"Automating without understanding is delegating responsibility without accountability."
Loss of agency
Teams no longer understand why decisions are made
Blind optimization
Maximizing metrics without understanding tradeoffs
Faster is not always better
Risk
Risk
14 / 20

The Shallow Learning Trap

If AI executes improvements automatically, humans may never learn why certain changes work.

Deep Learning (Good)
Teams understand why things work. Knowledge transfers between people. Organization can adapt to new contexts. Judgment improves over time.
Shallow Learning (Danger)
AI improves metrics, humans don't know why. Knowledge stays in the system, not the team. Organization becomes dependent on tools. Human judgment atrophies.
The difference between using AI and depending on AI
Risk

15 / 20

Preserving Human Judgment

Complex systems require judgment that AI cannot provide.

Direction
Deciding what to optimize for. Choosing which metrics matter. Defining success.
Guardrails
Setting boundaries on what AI can do. Defining ethical limits. Preventing harmful optimization.
Override
The ability to say no. To stop, reverse, or redirect when AI recommendations feel wrong.
Judgment cannot be delegated
